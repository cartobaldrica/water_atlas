Groundwater

Groundwater is as important as it is mysterious. Its underground nature makes groundwater one of the most difficult aspects of Oregon's water resources to understand and measure. Despite its importance and widespread use, there are a lot holes in out knowledge regarding groundwater in Oregon. Part of this relates to the difficulty and complexity of obtaining quality information about groundwater, and part of it relates to the age of a lot of the data that do exist. Groundwater may be mysterious, but fully understanding its intricacies will be necessary in order to adequately plan for the future.

















Aquifers and Recharge

Groundwater recharge is notoriously difficult to measure. Factors affecting recharge rates are numerous and variable, typically creating a necessity for extrapolation and modeling. The recharge data in this map was modeled in 2002 by the USGS in terms of estimated average groundwater recharge in millimeters per year.

Although recharge is independant of aquifer type, differences in aquifers affect the ease of groundwater extraction for human uses. Thus, the map combines the modeled recharge rate with the three aquifer categories defined by the USGS in Oregon.

Aquifer Types:

Unconsolidated Sand and Gravel

Sand and gravel aquifers exist in lowlands and river valleys. Consisting of fine-grained sediments, sand and gravel aquifers are caused by faulting, erosion or both. They are shallow and typically easy to access, which is reflected in their widespread use in public, domestic and industrial settings.

Volcanic

Central and Eastern Oregon are dominated by basalt flows and volcanoes, resulting in wide swaths of aquifers with volcanic origin. In basalt flows, joints between rock allow water to flow through freely, making them the most productive of volcanic-type aquifers. Regardless, permeability and ease of access in volcanic aquifers is largely variable.

Other Rock Types

Other aquifers in Oregon consist of various rock types and are common in the mountainous areas surrounding the Coast Range and the Wallowas. These formations share old age, and some have a marine origin. They are among the least productive aquifers in the state, and are typically only tapped for groundwater out of necessity.

Irrigation and Aquifers

89.7% of total groundwater extraction is used for irrigation. Each point on the map represents an irrigation water right. The points are combined with aquifer type, highlighting the dramatic reliance on unconsolidated sand and gravel aquifers.

With so much reliance on specific aquifers, especially in the drought-prone regions of Eastern Oregon, the question of groundwater sustainability becomes paramount. However, measuring the total capacity of an aquifer, especially one with multiple extraction points, is still a difficult and unreliable process.

County Use

The USGS's National Water Use Information Program compiles water use data by county every five years. Water use data is categorized by both its purpose (irrigation, domestic, mining, etc.) and its type (surface or ground, fresh or saline). Data are collected in terms of millions of gallons extracted per day.

Groundwater use has a vastly different distribution than surface water use, and highlights which regions of Oregon rely primarily on groundwater. However, the seasonality of water use isn't reflected in USGS compilations. For example, the average daily rate of irrigation over an entire year will always be lower than the daily rate of irrigation during summer.

Groundwater Conservation

Critical Groundwater Area

In 1965, the Oregon state legislature established the critical groundwater program as a legal framework for groundwater conservation. Critical groundwater areas are created in areas where it is estimated that excessive extraction has resulted in declining groundwater levels. Established critical groundwater areas face restrictions on withdrawal rates and limits on the ability to obtain new groundwater permits.

Groundwater Limited Area

The groundwater limited area program was established in 1992. It is designed to conserve groundwater in areas where groundwater levels are at risk of falling and to combat general resource depletion. Restrictions in groundwater limited areas are often not as severe as they are in critical groundwater areas.

Groundwater Management

In an effort to conserve groundwater quality, the Oregon DEQ established the Groundwater Management Area program. GWMAs are formed in areas that observe increased contaminant levels at least partially originating from nonpoint sources. The three designated GWMAs in Oregon were all created in response to increased nitrate levels. Local management committees are formed in affected areas and work with state agencies to produce action plans to reduce contaminant levels.
